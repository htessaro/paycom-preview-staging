Learn how charges represent successful payment authorizations and captures.

## Understanding charges
A **Charge** is the financial record created after a payment session is successfully completed. It represents the actual movement of money, or the intent to move it, from the customer to your account. While a payment session is a temporary container for the customer's journey, a charge is a permanent record used for accounting, refunds, and reporting.

Depending on your business model, a charge can exist in different states. For example, an immediate payment results in a "captured" charge, whereas a pre-authorization creates a "hold" charge that waits for your final confirmation.

## Charge lifecycle
Once the orchestration engine processes a transaction, the charge moves through these stages:

* **Authorized:** The customer's bank approved the transaction and reserved funds, but money hasn't moved yet
* **Captured:** The transaction is finalized and funds are sent for settlement (usually immediately after authorization, unless using a Hold flow)
* **Refunded:** All or part of the funds returned to the customer
* **Failed:** The transaction couldn't complete due to bank declines, technical errors, or fraud prevention

## Capture options
You can decide how you want to collect funds based on the type of products or services you provide:

* Immediate capture: This is the most common flow for digital goods or instant services. When a customer pays, the system authorizes and captures the funds in a single step. This minimizes manual work and ensures the payment is processed as quickly as possible.

* Delayed capture (Hold): For businesses that ship physical goods, you might prefer to hold the funds first. This ensures the customer has the money available without actually taking it until the item is ready to ship. You can then trigger the capture through the Dashboard or the API once the order is fulfilled. Also, if you decide not to fulfill an order, you can cancel the hold. This releases the reserved funds back to the customer's card immediately, which is often faster and easier than processing a formal refund.

## Charge operations
Use these endpoints to manage your transactions:

* **[Create a charge](/docs/api-reference/charges/create-charge):** Moves funds from a customer to a merchant account using raw card details, stored tokens, or digital wallets.
* **[Confirm a charge](/docs/api-reference/charges/confirm-charge):** Manually finalizes an authorization when using advanced flows like `confirm: false`.
* **[Get a charge](/docs/api-reference/charges/get-charge):** Retrieves details for a specific charge, including the `amount_refunded` and remaining capturable balance.
* **[Get charges](/docs/api-reference/charges/get-charges):** Returns a paginated list of all charges associated with your account.
* **[Get charges by reference](/docs/api-reference/charges/get-charge-by-reference):** Finds a charge using your internal merchant reference ID.
* **[Update a charge](/docs/api-reference/charges/update-charge):** Modifies specific metadata or editable fields of an existing charge record.

## Handling refunds
Refunds return funds to a customer for a previously captured charge. You can perform full or partial refunds. The orchestration engine communicates with the original processor to return funds, typically to the customer's original payment method.
To learn more, see [Refunds](/docs/payments/04-payment-concepts/06-refunds).
